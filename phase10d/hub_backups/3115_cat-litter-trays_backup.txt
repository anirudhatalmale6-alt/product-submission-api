<p class="has-text-align-center has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-815268a0be985f14f2e7a2f235fcddc2 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#4a4a4a;margin-top:16px;margin-bottom:16px;font-size:18px">Choosing the right cat litter tray comes down to your cat&#8217;s size, your home layout, cleaning preferences, and any specific health or behavioural needs. A well-chosen tray promotes consistent use and keeps your home fresh.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:group -->
<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-d701d537c09d36931d8cc14330c5c2fe" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">📏 Choose the Right Size</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-6dff5e40d60ce19880f9a556c3f69b02 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">The tray should be at least 1.5 times the length of your cat from nose to base of tail. A tray that is too small will be uncomfortable and may cause your cat to eliminate outside the box.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-e890c43ffcfeb759ed874c21d8c37ba6" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li>Standard adult cats need a tray measuring at least <strong>50 cm x 40 cm</strong>.</li>
<li>Large breeds like <strong>Maine Coons</strong> need extra-large trays — 60 cm x 45 cm or bigger.</li>
<li>Kittens can start with a smaller, low-sided tray and transition to full-size by 6 months.</li>
<li>In multi-cat homes, provide <strong>one tray per cat plus one extra</strong> to prevent resource guarding.</li>
</ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->
</div>
<!-- /wp:group -->

<!-- wp:group -->
<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-38cfb16e5b521476736e375c9cfb9a98" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🏠 Open vs Hooded Design</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-428fd31adf9eb7a45d396dd62f3ae9c8 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">The choice between open and hooded trays depends on your cat&#8217;s personality and your household environment. Each design has distinct advantages.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-d4de5cf7dfb64dfa29ea4631d940e665" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li><strong>Open trays:</strong> Better ventilation, easier to clean, preferred by anxious cats who need to see their surroundings.</li>
<li><strong>Hooded trays:</strong> Contain odour and scatter, offer privacy, ideal for busy homes with children or dogs.</li>
<li><strong>Top-entry trays:</strong> Excellent scatter control, dog-proof, but not suitable for senior or mobility-impaired cats.</li>
<li>If unsure, start with an open tray — most cats accept them readily.</li>
</ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->
</div>

<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-0c862fec1f492d8a0ccb4509eb59d442" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🧹 Consider Ease of Cleaning</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-160d4acd8b60a3a207a2da55f4aeae77 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Regular cleaning is essential for your cat&#8217;s health. Choose a tray that fits your lifestyle and cleaning routine.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-5f3450c14251b10e171303d87c47d213" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li>Choose trays with <strong>smooth, non-stick interiors</strong> that wipe clean easily.</li>
<li><strong>Sifting trays</strong> save time — lift, sift, and separate waste in seconds.</li>
<li><strong>Self-cleaning models</strong> are ideal if you struggle to scoop daily.</li>
<li>Replace plastic trays every <strong>6–12 months</strong> as scratches harbour bacteria and odour.</li>
<li><strong>Scoop at least twice daily</strong> — ammonia and hydrogen sulphide off-gas almost immediately, so frequent cleaning is the most effective odour control strategy.</li>
<li>Use <strong>activated carbon or charcoal additives</strong> to neutralise odours rather than masking them with heavily perfumed litters that can deter cats.</li>
<li><strong>Weekly full clean:</strong> empty all used litter, wash the tray with warm water and mild unscented detergent, rinse thoroughly, and dry completely before adding fresh litter.</li>
<li><strong>Monthly inspection:</strong> check for scratches, cracks, or damage that harbour bacteria — deep-clean with a pet-safe disinfectant and allow to air dry fully.</li>
<li>Maintain a <strong>litter depth of 5–7 cm</strong> and ensure good ventilation in the tray room to reduce lingering smells.</li>
<li>Monitor your cat&#8217;s litter habits — <strong>changes in frequency, unusual odour, or blood</strong> are early warning signs of urinary tract infections, kidney disease, or diabetes.</li>
</ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-7a144a9a4172f2223453bc42c45b554b" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🐱 Think About Your Cat&#8217;s Preferences</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-08cb5f658af3a0560b66602637ea1575 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Every cat has individual preferences around their toileting environment. Observing your cat&#8217;s behaviour is the best guide to choosing the right tray.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-6c01b05deff7d1be0495018802e627ee" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li>Cats that <strong>dig vigorously</strong> need high-sided trays to contain scatter.</li>
<li><strong>Shy or nervous cats</strong> may prefer a hooded tray for privacy and security.</li>
<li><strong>Senior cats</strong> need trays with a low-cut entry point for easy access.</li>
<li>If your cat suddenly stops using the tray, try a different style or location before assuming a medical issue.</li>
<li><strong>Placement matters:</strong> keep trays away from food and water bowls — cats refuse to eat and toilet in the same area. Avoid high-traffic zones and spots near loud appliances.</li>
<li>In <strong>multi-storey homes</strong>, place at least one tray on each floor so your cat always has easy access.</li>
<li>For <strong>multi-cat households</strong>, spread trays across different rooms — a row of trays in one location is seen by cats as a single large tray, defeating the purpose of multiple options.</li>
<li>Quiet <strong>bathrooms, utility rooms, and spare bedrooms</strong> are consistently popular locations for cat litter trays.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-left alignwide has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-f8e01996ff9984867b4224fe6dc4d14d" id="cat-litter-trays-faq" style="color:#1a1a1a;margin-bottom:25px;font-size:32px;font-style:normal;font-weight:700;line-height:1.2">Cat Litter Trays FAQ</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>How many cat litter trays do I need?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">The general recommendation is one cat litter tray per cat, plus one extra. For a single cat household, having two trays is ideal. For two cats, three trays are recommended, and so on. This ensures every cat always has access to a clean tray and reduces the risk of resource guarding or territorial behaviour around the litter area.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>How often should I replace cat litter trays?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Plastic trays should typically be replaced every six to twelve months, or sooner if they become heavily scratched. Scratches in the plastic create microscopic grooves where bacteria and odour-causing compounds can accumulate and cannot be fully cleaned away. Stainless steel cat litter trays last considerably longer and can be an economical long-term investment.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>Why is my cat not using the litter tray?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">There are many possible reasons a cat may avoid using a litter tray, including: the tray is not being cleaned frequently enough; the litter type has changed; the tray is in a stressful or noisy location; the tray is too small; a medical condition such as a urinary tract infection is causing pain; or stress from a change in the household. Always consult your vet first to rule out health issues, then work through environmental and litter-related factors systematically.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>What depth of litter should I put in cat litter trays?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most cats prefer a litter depth of approximately 5–7 cm. This gives them enough material to dig and cover their waste effectively, which is an important part of their natural behaviour. Too little litter and your cat may refuse to use the tray or waste will reach the bottom of the tray and stick. Too much litter can cause excessive scatter and may make some cats uncomfortable.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>Should I use a liner in my cat litter trays?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Litter tray liners can make cleaning easier by allowing you to lift out all the used litter in one go, but they are not universally accepted by cats. Some cats dislike the feel or crinkle of plastic liners and may scratch or shred them, which defeats the purpose. If you want to try liners, introduce them gradually and if your cat rejects them, do not force the issue.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>Can I use cat litter trays for kittens?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Yes, a standard tray is suitable for kittens, but choose a smaller option with low sides to start so the kitten can climb in and out easily. Very young kittens under 8 weeks may need extra-low sides. As the kitten grows, transition them gradually to a full-size tray. Avoid hooded designs for very young kittens as the enclosed space can be confusing and intimidating.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>What is the best way to dispose of used cat litter?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">In the UK, most used cat litter should be bagged securely and placed in your general waste bin — it cannot go in food waste or garden waste collections due to the risk of toxoplasmosis from cat faeces. Some biodegradable litters such as wood pellet or paper-based options can be composted in a dedicated compost bin. Never flush cat litter down the toilet, as it can damage plumbing and water treatment systems.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<p class="has-text-align-center has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-c154de3808ca158b69a512e15fdfd767 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#4a4a4a;margin-top:16px;margin-bottom:30px;font-size:18px">Beyond choosing the right tray, these practical guides will help you get the most out of your cat litter setup — from picking the best litter type to training a new kitten.</p>

<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">

<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-c1793d9afdfc2642cebe6e962277112e" id="complete-guide-to-cat-litter-types" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">Complete Guide to Cat Litter Types</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-9d58efdef2261405fa4a58a95cf1cc9a wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Choosing the right litter for your cat litter trays is just as important as choosing the tray itself. The four most common types available in the UK are clumping clay, silica gel crystals, wood pellets, and recycled paper.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-0405f92f95cfd6d5783af4bc90a7da90" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li><strong>Clumping clay litter</strong> — the most popular choice, forming solid clumps when wet for quick scooping and excellent odour control.</li>
<li><strong>Silica gel crystals</strong> — absorb moisture and can last up to four weeks between full changes, making them economical despite a higher upfront cost.</li>
<li><strong>Wood pellet litter</strong> — 100% biodegradable, made from compressed sawdust with a naturally pleasant pine scent. The most eco-friendly option.</li>
<li><strong>Recycled paper litter</strong> — soft on paws and virtually dust-free, ideal for cats recovering from surgery or those with respiratory sensitivities.</li>
<li>Ensure your cat accepts the texture — some cats are particular about substrate and may reject an unfamiliar litter type. Transition gradually by mixing old and new litter over a week.</li>
</ul>

<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-fa05a791894940b307dc0a9cd0a9a928" id="how-to-litter-train-a-kitten" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">How to Litter Train a Kitten</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-945670fd7520fe47a30618fc2ef1b424 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Most kittens take to cat litter trays instinctively, but a gentle introduction helps establish good habits early. Here is a step-by-step approach to successful litter training.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-2c973851c5d4d82ee18129afe72af0ed" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li><strong>Introduce early:</strong> place your kitten directly into the tray after meals, after naps, and any time you observe sniffing or circling behaviour.</li>
<li><strong>Start with a low-sided tray</strong> so the kitten can climb in easily, and keep it in a consistent, accessible location away from food and water.</li>
<li><strong>Never punish accidents</strong> — simply clean the area with an enzymatic cleaner and redirect them to their tray.</li>
<li>Kittens have small bladders and may need access to trays <strong>every 30 to 60 minutes</strong> during active periods.</li>
<li>Consistency and patience are the keys to success — <strong>most kittens are fully trained within one to two weeks</strong> of consistent use.</li>
<li>Avoid hooded designs for very young kittens as the enclosed space can be confusing and intimidating.</li>
</ul>
