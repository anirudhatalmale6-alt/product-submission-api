<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-173d781f7a5f565540a2c2f823cf53f3" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">📏 Consider Your Cat&#8217;s Size</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-3851503d7feacb72960d77781a5148fe wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">The scratching post must be tall enough for your cat to stretch to full length. A post that&#8217;s too short will be ignored in favour of your furniture.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-d163dbfe2550f2265b3db170d71726e3" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li>For most adult cats, choose a post <strong>at least 60–70 cm tall</strong>.</li>
<li>Large breeds like <strong>Maine Coons and Ragdolls</strong> need 80–100 cm or taller.</li>
<li>Kittens can start with smaller posts, but <strong>they&#8217;ll outgrow them quickly</strong>.</li>
<li>Always check the <strong>base width</strong> — it should be at least as wide as the post is tall for stability.</li>
</ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->
</div>
<!-- /wp:group -->

<!-- wp:group -->
<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-29e3023b55b1878b80640b25853a71a9" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🪵 Choose the Right Material</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-f8d8a671cf09ac683ca2ea40f2ee642e wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Different cats have strong preferences for scratching surfaces. Offering the right material dramatically increases the chances your cat will use their post.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-800f4da5a730e1e6933857cc9227a80a" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li><strong>Sisal rope/fabric:</strong> The gold standard — coarse, durable, and universally loved by cats.</li>
<li><strong>Corrugated cardboard:</strong> Affordable, satisfying texture, ideal for horizontal scratchers.</li>
<li><strong>Carpet:</strong> Budget-friendly but can reinforce carpet-scratching habits elsewhere.</li>
<li><strong>Natural wood/bark:</strong> Eco-friendly and instinctive — mimics scratching trees outdoors.</li>
</ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-c36c469126f0b70584cf5c9bfffcc945 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6"><strong>Sisal</strong> is a natural plant fibre with a coarse, rope-like texture that cats find enormously satisfying. Sisal-wrapped <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> fray in a way that mimics bark, appealing to cats&#8217; instincts. A good sisal post can last two to four years with regular use.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-ee8520eeac7eb47540f7f4cd61b4695d wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6"><strong>Corrugated cardboard</strong> creates a deeply satisfying scratching experience and is the most affordable option. The downside is that it wears through quickly and sheds debris, so plan for regular replacements every one to four months.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-ad0cccf19c657b8ea41f3bfb27574c8d wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6"><strong>Natural wood and bark</strong> — particularly birch logs — are increasingly popular for eco-conscious cat owners. Cats evolved to scratch trees, and many take to natural wood surfaces with great enthusiasm. They can be found at garden centres as well as pet shops.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-ad0e1e1e14a0e8095f27b3946689bddf" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🏠 Think About Placement</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-70ffdf7d652c24bd1c8266fc38e99828 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Even the best scratching post will be ignored if placed in the wrong spot. Cats scratch where it matters to them — usually in prominent, social areas.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-e1e501ef0917311c0137b97cfd88c19c" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li>Place posts <strong>near sleeping areas</strong> — cats scratch when they wake up.</li>
<li>Position in <strong>main living areas</strong> where your cat spends the most time.</li>
<li>If your cat scratches furniture, place the post <strong>right next to the targeted spot</strong>.</li>
<li>In multi-storey homes, provide <strong>at least one post per floor</strong>.</li>
</ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-b35d644c694de0e785d62e2e613598b2 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:12px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6"><strong>Small spaces need creative solutions.</strong> Wall-mounted scratchers are the ultimate space-saver — they fix directly to the wall and take up zero floor space. You can mount them at the precise height that suits your cat and combine them with wall shelves for a complete vertical playground.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-eed5264cecd5148755b7a9ebc00a75d1 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6"><strong>Corner scratching posts</strong> fit snugly into room corners, protecting the exact spots cats tend to target while using minimal floor area. <strong>Door-hanging scratchers</strong> hook over any standard door with no permanent installation — ideal for renters who cannot drill into walls.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-b726cc1a5d8eb945947d78b3f37626ac wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Even a simple <strong>flat cardboard scratcher pad</strong> can slide under furniture when not in use, providing an affordable, replaceable scratching option for the smallest of homes.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-01aeb11526b0f1647f78aeeaf5cd3b08" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🐱 Match Your Cat&#8217;s Scratching Style</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-3383d1e3812d7ae56ba166844b35ba56 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Observe how your cat scratches — their preferred orientation and surface will guide you to the best type of post.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-7d08924694d3b6acf25a9c7e2113317d" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li><strong>Vertical scratchers</strong> (door frames, sofa sides) need tall upright sisal posts.</li>
<li><strong>Horizontal scratchers</strong> (carpets, rugs) prefer flat cardboard pads or mats.</li>
<li><strong>Angle scratchers</strong> benefit from inclined ramp-style scratchers.</li>
<li>Many cats enjoy <strong>multiple orientations</strong> — offer variety for the best results.</li>
</ul>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-center has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-5e6f09264bb3e994d261e4a2d3a2371a" id="cat-scratching-posts-faq" style="color:#222222;padding-top:22px;padding-bottom:22px;font-size:32px;font-style:normal;font-weight:700">Cat Scratching Posts FAQ</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>How many cat scratching posts does one cat need?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Most cat behaviour experts recommend at least two <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> per cat: one vertical post in a main living area and one horizontal pad near a sleeping spot. If your home has multiple floors, each floor should have at least one <strong>cat scratching post</strong>. Having more <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> is rarely a problem — having too few almost always is.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>How often should I replace a cat scratching post?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">A sisal-wrapped <strong>cat scratching post</strong> used by one cat typically lasts one to three years before the covering is worn through. Cardboard horizontal pads may need replacing every one to four months depending on use. Check your <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> regularly — when the material is smooth, shredded to the core, or no longer engaging your cat, it&#8217;s time to replace it. Don&#8217;t wait until the post is completely destroyed, as a worn-out <strong>cat scratching post</strong> is less effective at claw maintenance.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>My cat ignores the cat scratching post — what should I do?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">First, check the location: a scratching post tucked in a corner is likely to be ignored. Move it to a prominent spot near where the cat sleeps or spends time. Second, check the height — if the post doesn&#8217;t allow a full stretch, try a taller one. Third, try a different material: if your post is carpet-covered, try sisal. If sisal isn&#8217;t working, try a cardboard pad. Finally, use catnip or silvervine to spark interest. Hanging a <a href="/cat-toys/">cat toy</a> from the top of a vertical <strong>cat scratching post</strong> can also encourage a cat to reach up and start scratching.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>Are cat scratching posts safe for kittens?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Yes — introducing <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> to kittens early is one of the best habits you can establish. Kittens start scratching at just a few weeks old, so having a small, stable <strong>cat scratching post</strong> available from the moment they arrive home helps build good habits from the start. Make sure the base is stable enough that a playful kitten can&#8217;t knock the post over — a toppled <strong>cat scratching post</strong> can frighten a kitten and put them off using it permanently.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>Do declawed cats still need cat scratching posts?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Declawing is illegal in the UK under the Animal Welfare Act 2006, so this is less of a concern for UK cat owners. However, cats that have been declawed abroad still exhibit scratching behaviour — they go through the motions even without claws, as it&#8217;s an ingrained instinct. Declawed cats should still be provided with <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> to allow them to stretch, mark, and self-soothe, though softer materials like carpet or fleece are more comfortable for them than sisal.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>Can I use cat scratching post deterrent spray on furniture?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Yes. Deterrent sprays use citrus or bitter scents that cats find unpleasant, making treated furniture less attractive as a scratching target. They work best when used alongside <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> placed nearby — deterring the furniture while simultaneously offering an appealing alternative. Always test sprays on a hidden patch of fabric first, as some formulations can affect upholstery colours. Also check out our <a href="/cat-grooming/">cat grooming</a> section for advice on regular nail trimming, which reduces the urgency of scratching and protects furniture.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<!-- wp:details -->
<details class="wp-block-details alignwide has-border-color has-ast-global-color-4-background-color has-background is-layout-flow wp-block-details-is-layout-flow" style="border-color:#e5e5e5;border-width:8px;margin-bottom:12px;padding-top:20px;padding-bottom:20px;font-size:18px">
<summary>What is the best cat scratching post for a large cat?</summary>
<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="wp-block-paragraph">Large breeds need tall, heavy-duty <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> — look for a minimum height of 80–90 cm and a very wide, weighted base. Sisal-wrapped posts with a diameter of at least 10 cm are more satisfying for large cats than thin posts, which can feel unstable under their weight. Maine Coons, Ragdolls, and Norwegian Forest Cats in particular benefit from extra-tall <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> or full cat trees with robust scratching columns.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->
</details>
<!-- /wp:details -->

<h2 class="wp-block-heading has-text-align-center has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-1b505b7a1589fbbbcde1701117d172da" id="useful-tips-for-every-pet-owner" style="color:#222222;padding-top:22px;padding-bottom:22px;font-size:32px;font-style:normal;font-weight:700">Useful Tips for Every Pet Owner</h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-align-center has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-1d6e6daf50963f8374f92dad2d617995 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#444444;margin-bottom:30px;font-size:18px;line-height:1.6">From training your cat to use a scratching post to building your own DIY version, these practical tips will help you get the most out of your purchase.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:group -->
<div class="wp-block-group has-border-color has-background is-layout-constrained wp-container-core-group-is-layout-d06944bd wp-block-group-is-layout-constrained" style="border-color:#0073aa;border-width:3px;border-top-left-radius:12px;border-top-right-radius:12px;border-bottom-left-radius:12px;border-bottom-right-radius:12px;background-color:#ffffff;margin-bottom:28px;padding-top:28px;padding-right:28px;padding-bottom:28px;padding-left:28px">
<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-d8dd9bbc2421a8b6ca92597199ed7a97" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🎓 How to Train Your Cat to Use a Scratching Post</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-abf25130277acb9a8ff28a4ae5507f32 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">Most cats will investigate a new <strong>cat scratching post</strong> fairly quickly, especially if it&#8217;s placed correctly and made from an appealing material. However, some cats — particularly those who have been scratching furniture for a long time — may need a little encouragement.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-ee7bf7ea9575d803f62b8d509f5540ea" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li><strong>Step 1: Make it irresistible.</strong> Rub dried catnip into the surface or hang a favourite <a href="/cat-toys/">cat toy</a> from the top. Placing the post near where the cat already spends time increases the likelihood of a first investigation.</li>
<li><strong>Step 2: Reward any interaction.</strong> The moment your cat sniffs, rubs against, or scratches the post, reward them immediately with a treat and verbal praise. You&#8217;re building a positive association between the <strong>cat scratching post</strong> and good things happening.</li>
<li><strong>Step 3: Redirect, don&#8217;t punish.</strong> If you catch your cat scratching the sofa, calmly carry them to the nearest <strong>cat scratching post</strong>. Gently place their paws on the surface. Never shout at or punish a cat for scratching — punishment creates anxiety, which often increases scratching behaviour.</li>
<li><strong>Step 4: Make unwanted surfaces less appealing.</strong> Temporarily protect furniture with double-sided sticky tape, aluminium foil, or scratch-deterrent sprays. Most of these solutions are only needed for a few weeks while the cat learns to prefer their new posts.</li>
<li><strong>Step 5: Be patient and consistent.</strong> Training can take anywhere from a few days to a few weeks. Consistency is key — every time you redirect your cat and reward them, you reinforce the behaviour. Most cats are fully redirected within a month.</li>
</ul>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-257476ee9ea70c1ab1b9338b502c88cb" style="color:#0073aa;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:20px;font-style:normal;font-weight:600">🔨 DIY Cat Scratching Post Ideas</h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p class="has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-9c3316e91e072a2da285cead70e3fcb3 wp-block-paragraph" style="color:#333333;margin-top:1px;margin-bottom:12px;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">If you&#8217;re handy with basic tools, making your own <strong>cat scratching posts</strong> is a rewarding and cost-effective project. Homemade posts can be customised exactly to your cat&#8217;s size preferences and your home&#8217;s décor.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list has-text-color has-link-color wp-elements-a6de9220ad9ddea3a71a62415d5c2d51" style="color:#333333;font-size:16px;line-height:1.6">
<li><strong>Simple sisal post:</strong> A piece of 4×4 timber (at least 70 cm tall), a heavy base board, wood screws, and a roll of sisal rope. Sand the timber smooth, fix it vertically to the base, then wrap tightly with sisal from bottom to top, securing each end with a staple gun. Cover the base with carpet to prevent sliding — a sturdy post for under £20.</li>
<li><strong>Wall-mounted post:</strong> Fix fence posts to wall brackets for a space-saving vertical scratcher. Or fashion horizontal pads from offcuts of sisal matting stapled to a backing board.</li>
<li><strong>Natural branch scratcher:</strong> Natural tree branches — particularly birch or apple wood — can be mounted at an angle to create a natural-looking <strong>cat scratching post</strong> that doubles as décor.</li>
<li><strong>Easy repairs:</strong> The main advantage of DIY posts is repairability. When the sisal wears through, simply unwrap the old rope and rewrap with new. This extends the life of a well-built frame almost indefinitely, making it a sustainable and economical choice.</li>
</ul>
